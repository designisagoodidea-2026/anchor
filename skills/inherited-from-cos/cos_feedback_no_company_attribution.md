**Rule:** Never make declarative claims about what a company is, stands for, or is built for in any output drafted on Jason's behalf. Don't author the organization's identity, strategy, brand, or purpose. If engaging with what they've said about themselves, **quote them** using their phrasing — never paraphrase their strategy into your own words. Or sidestep attribution entirely and stay in Jason's first-person experience and stance.

**What the rule does NOT cover (clarified 2026-05-28):** Naming a prior or current employer is fine — "At Netflix I did X" / "I led research at Walmart's Global Design organization" is not company-attribution, it's biographical fact. The rule kicks in when you describe their *strategy, mission, identity, needs, or future direction* in your own words. Employer-name discretion (when to generalize "global design organization" vs. name "Walmart") is governed separately by [[cos-feedback-employer-discretion]] — that's a different lens about internal detail sensitivity, not a blanket rule against naming.

**Why:** It reads as presumptuous and is often inaccurate. Jason doesn't actually know the internal strategy of any company he's applying to or networking with, and the senior leaders reading these messages do. Even when the attribution is flattering and roughly correct, the move lands as a tonal misstep — the recipient's filter registers "this person is telling me what we do" and the rest of the message gets discounted. Established 2026-05-27 after the Google Payments cover letter draft contained "The throughline is the one Google Pay is built for..." Jason caught it and named the rule.

**How to apply:**

1. **Don't write:** "X is built for Y" / "Your strategy is Z" / "What [Company] stands for is W" / "Your mission to [paraphrased mission] aligns with my values."
2. **Don't paraphrase their mission into your own words.** Even if the paraphrase is accurate, it's still authoring their identity.
3. **If you want to engage with something they've said about themselves, quote them directly,** with their phrasing. Use citation-style references, not absorption-style restatements.
4. **Cite specific named sources where possible:** a recruiter's outreach language, a published post, a leader's talk. "Justin Fay described Rachel Kobetz's design transformation in his InMail" is fine — that's attributing to a real person who said real words.
5. **Or sidestep entirely.** Stay in Jason's first-person experience, his observations, his throughline. The argument should always be JASON'S argument, not the company's.

**Examples (bad → good):**

BAD: "The throughline is the one Google Pay is built for: where strategy, regulation, and human practice all have to operate at once."
GOOD: "The throughline across all three is where strategy, regulation, and human practice all have to operate at once." (drops the attribution; Jason owns the throughline)

BAD: "Your mission to empower creators aligns with my values."
GOOD: skip the line entirely OR quote the actual mission language verbatim and respond to it specifically.

BAD: "PayPal is in the middle of a multi-year transformation evolving into a true platform for global commerce."
GOOD: "I've been hearing about Rachel Kobetz's design transformation from a different angle through Kristin Skinner, who's been a coach of mine for two years." (cites a real warm-contact channel rather than authoring PayPal's strategy)

BAD: "At Home, the work is about building an integrated ecosystem of devices, software, and AI-powered experiences."
GOOD: skip — that's the JD. Don't restate it back. (See [[cos-feedback-no-generic-outputs]] for the related restating-JD-back rule.)

**Softer forms of the same defect (added 2026-05-28 after the Google XR Studio cover letter draft).** The egregious form is "Company X is built for Y." But the same defect lives in softer phrasings that *characterize the team's needs, problems, or focus areas*. Watch for these — they're easier to miss because they sound humble:

BAD: "exactly the kind of cross-disciplinary operating model this Studio needs"
GOOD: "twenty-five years of building cross-disciplinary operating models" (drop the framing about what the team needs; let the reader judge fit)

BAD: "the operating challenge of a new, multi-disciplinary team building toward a new modality is the explicit shape of the work"
GOOD: "the role sits at the intersection of the operational craft I've built and the modality I've been pulled toward for a long time" (Jason's stance about the role, not a characterization of what the team is or what it's doing)

BAD: "they bear on what an XR Studio will need to figure out about AI-assisted asset generation, demo production, and documentation"
GOOD: "the questions they were probing — AI as collaborator in generating, translating, and documenting design work — are still open" (Jason's reflection on what the prior work was probing; no claim about what THIS team needs to figure out)

**Pattern smell-test:** if the sentence contains "the kind of X this [team / Studio / company] needs" / "what they'll need to figure out" / "what their challenge is" / "the work they're doing is" — you're authoring their identity in a quiet voice. Rewrite in first person about *Jason's* experience, stance, or work.

**Borderline cases:**

- **Paraphrasing their own published mission statement is borderline.** If you can quote them directly, do. If you paraphrase, do it lightly and only when the paraphrase is verifiable against published material. Default to quoting.
- **Matching Jason's value to their published mission is fine when carefully scoped.** Example from the Home draft: "The idea that a home is a place where the people inside it deserve to be cared for is what drew me to Airbnb in 2017, and it's the same idea I read in how Home describes itself." That's Jason's value + a citation of Home's published language, not Jason authoring Home's identity.
- **Asking how they think about X is not the same as telling them X.** "I'm eager to learn more about how the team thinks about Y" is question-shaped, even if the question presumes Y is a thing. Marginal but acceptable. "How the team thinks about" leaves room for them to define their own approach.

**Applies to:**
- `cover-letter`
- `recruiter-outreach`
- `network-mapper` outreach drafts
- LinkedIn DM replies
- intro request drafts
- any communication where Jason might describe what a company does, is, or stands for

**Related:** [[cos-feedback-no-generic-outputs]] (don't restate the JD back; this is the same family of defect — both involve Jason putting words into the company's mouth or echoing what they've already said). This new rule is sharper on attribution specifically.
